Draft AI Guidelines

This document synthesizes AI guidance for the Climate Impacts Group (CIG) team. It covers five key themes. Click each theme below to expand and read the full guidance, then use the board to share your feedback.

Transparency is a foundational component to academic integrity and ethical use of AI. To maintain transparency in AI use, consider and acknowledge the what, how, and when of AI use.

Apply standard citation and attribution in all written documents, presentations, and publications — disclosing what AI tool was used, when it was used, and the step(s) in which it was used.
In publications, explain how you used AI in your work (e.g., data analysis, literature review, writing, idea generation, editing, or images/illustrations) — for example in a methods section.
Develop language for discussing AI use with partners and navigating any misalignments in AI use and comfort levels between CIG and partners.
Example disclosure "We used [AI Tool Name] to check grammar and improve language clarity in preparing this manuscript. No AI-generated content was included in the development of ideas, arguments, or conclusions." — Taylor and Francis Publishers, 2025
Key questions to consider
Is the above disclosure the correct or best approach?
To what extent is requiring the disclosure of the exact prompt useful?
Unless you can do it on your own, don't use AI to do the task — does this resonate?

Assume that AI companies will, at best, follow the minimum standard of the law — and may break laws or promises about data use. Never share private or sensitive data with AI, regardless of promises made about privacy.

1st priority: Exclusively use tools that don't share data with their company (a secure "lockbox"). UW Purple via UW NetID is an example — the UW provides a service to ensure the lockbox is secured and the company adheres to standards.
2nd priority: If lockbox tools aren't available, opt out of data sharing where that option exists. Note: this assumes the AI company will consistently adhere to promised behavior — less secure than option 1.
3rd priority: If 1 and 2 are not possible, take strong precaution. Assume the AI company may use, reproduce, replicate, and/or reformat your work without regard to privacy, accuracy, or attribution.
Use of exclusively non-proprietary data is low risk (e.g., asking AI to summarize peer-reviewed publications). Uploading unpublished manuscripts may undermine the authors' ownership — seek permission first.
CIG should consider adding a statement to publications and video recordings requesting they not be used for AI training.

At CIG, academic and scientific integrity means AI tools support, but never replace, human intellectual work, responsibility, and accountability. CIG staff and students listed as authors must make substantial human scholarly contributions.

AI systems cannot be authors or co-authors on any CIG products — they cannot take responsibility, provide informed consent, or be held accountable for research conduct or findings.
Uncritical copying of AI output is treated as plagiarism. Any use of AI for idea generation, analysis, writing, translation, coding, or proposal preparation must be clearly disclosed.
Prohibited use example: Asking AI to "write the methods and results," pasting in unpublished data, then copying AI-generated text without checking, verifying, or disclosing — this constitutes research misconduct at CIG.
Appropriate use example: Drafting a literature review yourself, then using UW Purple to check grammar and clarity, reviewing all edits, and adding a disclosure: "We used UW Purple for grammar checking and language refinement; all ideas, analyses, and conclusions were developed by the authors."
Supervisors and project leads must set clear expectations about acceptable AI use for all project staff. CIG will periodically revisit what counts as inappropriate use.

Human judgment remains essential. AI should augment, not replace, human expertise, oversight, and decision-making. Human oversight is required whenever decisions impact people — which applies to virtually all of CIG's work.

As with any source of information, users of AI should frequently question where knowledge is coming from and remain aware of known AI biases.
For early career staff, students, or staff learning a new skill: ensure you understand how to perform a task first, then use AI to automate or iterate — so you can provide robust oversight.
The more intensively AI tools are used, the greater the degree of oversight needed. AI is best used as a supportive tool, not a key or sole decision-making tool.
Recognize AI's limitations in language (e.g., confusing technical terminology with different meanings across fields), cultural context, and nuance.
Ensure ethical, reflexive, and bias-aware AI use through continuous reflection on power, privilege, data bias, community impact, and model limitations — this area is likely to evolve quickly.

AI should be guided by overarching principles of justice, accountability, respect for privacy, transparency, beneficence, assessment of risks and benefits, and nondiscrimination. Impacts should be continuously monitored and reevaluated.

Bias / Fairness: AI is only as fair as its training data. Organizations need to adopt ways to watch for outputs that reproduce implicit or explicit biases from existing knowledge sources.
Research ethics: AI cannot be a co-author. Clear attribution of text, data, intellectual property, and consent are critical. Authors must ensure accuracy, originality, and alignment with scholarly ethical principles.
Transparency / Model provenance: Users should disclose what model was used, where it came from, and its limitations — including understanding terms of service and training data origins for each tool.
Prohibited uses: AI must not be used for explicitly illegal purposes including hate speech, harassment, malware creation, or fraud.
Environmental issues: AI has a significant environmental footprint; usage should be intentional and purposeful. A means of comparing energy/water use intensity across models would be helpful for CIG staff.
Equity / access: AI should actively serve marginalized and underserved communities. Acknowledge communities bearing the environmental and economic burden of AI data center buildouts in AI use decisions.
Human oversight: AI must not be used without careful human oversight in high-stakes decisions — including peer review, funding allocation, and career advancement. Humans remain accountable for all AI-assisted outputs.
