Draft AI Guidelines

This document synthesizes AI guidance from 15 academic institutions, publishers, and federal granting bodies. It identifies five key themes for the Climate Impacts Group (CIG) team to discuss and provide feedback on.

Key Challenges

🌍
Environmental & climate impacts — AI has a significant energy and water footprint; use should be intentional.
🤔
Hallucinations — AI tools can confidently produce factually wrong outputs; fact-checking is critical.
🔲
Lack of transparency ("black box") — AI complexity makes it hard to explain how outputs are generated.
⚖️
Bias in outputs — AI reflects human biases in training data; outputs must be carefully reviewed.
©️
Copyright & intellectual property — AI is built on ingestion of copyrighted material; legal questions remain unresolved.
🔒
Data privacy & security — Input data may be used for AI training; never share private or sensitive data.
Theme 1 — Transparency

Transparency is foundational to academic integrity and ethical AI use. When using AI, disclose what tool was used, when, and how it was used.

Apply standard citation and attribution in all written documents, presentations, and publications disclosing AI use.
In publications, explain how AI was used — e.g., data analysis, literature review, writing, editing, or image generation — in a methods section or similar.
Develop language for discussing AI use with partners and navigating any misalignments in AI use and comfort levels between CIG and partners.
Example disclosure "We used [AI Tool Name] to check grammar and improve language clarity in preparing this manuscript. No AI-generated content was included in the development of ideas, arguments, or conclusions." — Taylor and Francis Publishers, 2025
Theme 2 — Data Privacy

Assume AI companies will follow the minimum standard of the law — and may break it. Never share private or sensitive data with AI tools.

1st priority: Use tools that don't share data with their company (a secure "lockbox"). UW Purple via UW NetID is an example.
2nd priority: If lockbox tools aren't available, opt out of data sharing where that option exists.
3rd priority: If neither is possible, use only non-proprietary data and assume the AI company may use, reproduce, or reformat your work without attribution.
Use of exclusively non-proprietary, publicly available data in AI tools is low-risk (e.g., asking AI to summarize peer-reviewed publications).
CIG should consider adding a policy statement to publications requesting they not be used for AI training.
Theme 3 — Academic Integrity

AI tools support but never replace human intellectual work, responsibility, and accountability. CIG staff and students listed as authors must make substantial scholarly contributions.

AI systems cannot be authors or co-authors on any CIG products — they cannot take responsibility or be held accountable for research conduct.
Uncritical copying of AI output is treated as plagiarism. All substantive AI use must be disclosed.
Prohibited use: Asking AI to "write the methods and results," then copying AI-generated text without checking, verifying, or disclosing constitutes research misconduct.
Appropriate use: Drafting work yourself, then using a lockbox AI tool to check grammar and clarity, reviewing all edits, and adding a disclosure in the methods section.
Supervisors and project leads must set clear expectations about acceptable AI use for all project staff.
Theme 4 — Human Validation

Human judgment is essential. AI should augment, not replace, human expertise, oversight, and decision-making. Human oversight is required whenever decisions impact people.

Researchers must integrate AI in ways that preserve critical thinking and methodological rigor.
The more intensively AI is used, the greater the degree of oversight needed. AI is best used as a supportive, not sole, decision-making tool.
Recognize AI's limitations in language, cultural context, and nuance — especially confusing technical terminology across fields.
For early career staff or those learning a new skill: understand how to perform a task first, then use AI to automate or iterate.
Engage in continuous reflection on power, privilege, data bias, community impact, and model limitations in AI use.
Theme 5 — Ethics

AI should be guided by principles of justice, accountability, transparency, beneficence, and nondiscrimination. Impacts should be continuously monitored and reevaluated.

Bias / Fairness: AI is only as fair as its training data. Organizations need to watch for outputs reproducing implicit or explicit biases.
Research ethics: AI cannot be a co-author. Guidance for attribution of text, data, intellectual property, and consent is critical.
Environmental issues: AI has a significant environmental footprint. Usage should be intentional; compare energy/water use across models when possible.
Equity / access: AI should actively serve marginalized and underserved communities. Acknowledge communities bearing the burden of AI data center buildouts in AI use decisions.
Human oversight: AI must not be used without careful human oversight in high-stakes decisions — including peer review, funding allocation, and career advancement.
Prohibited uses: AI must not be used for explicitly illegal purposes including hate speech, harassment, malware, or fraud.
